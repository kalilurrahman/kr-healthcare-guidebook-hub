Chapter 1: Healthcare Industry Architecture & The Digital Imperative

Executive Summary

Healthcare is the world's largest and most complex industry — a $4.9 trillion annual enterprise in the United States alone, serving 330 million Americans through an intricate web of providers, payers, pharmacy benefit managers, government programs, and technology platforms. Three in four American adults carry at least one chronic condition. Administrative costs consume 25–30 cents of every dollar spent on care. Denial rates average 12%, with 84% of those denials deemed potentially avoidable. Staffing shortages, payer friction, rising costs, and digital transformation expectations are converging simultaneously. These pressures do not have incremental solutions. They demand structural transformation — and digital capability is the engine of that transformation. This handbook is built for the leaders designing that transformation: GCC leaders, healthcare operations executives, transformation teams, strategy consultants, product leaders, and enterprise architects. It connects domain knowledge, operating models, metrics, technology, governance, and GCC capability design into one practical framework spanning the full healthcare value chain.

1.1 The Healthcare Ecosystem — A Connected Domain Map

Healthcare cannot be understood as isolated sectors. It operates as a connected system of enterprise domains, each dependent on the others, each generating and consuming data that flows across organizational boundaries.

The six primary domains:

Providers deliver care across hospitals, physician groups, ambulatory surgical centers, diagnostic labs, home health agencies, and post-acute facilities. Their business model depends on clinical quality, capacity utilization, payer reimbursement accuracy, and efficient revenue cycle execution. The average US hospital operates on a 2–3% operating margin — making revenue leakage through denials and underpayments an existential issue.

Payers — commercial insurers, Medicare Advantage plans, Medicaid managed care organizations — manage eligibility, benefits, authorizations, network participation, claims adjudication, and member engagement. UnitedHealth Group, Elevance Health, CVS/Aetna, Cigna, and Humana collectively manage health benefits for over 200 million Americans. Their operational complexity creates friction for providers, especially through prior authorization, denials, and policy variation.

Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) — led by CVS Caremark, Express Scripts (Evernorth/Cigna), and OptumRx — sit at the intersection of drug access, cost management, and outcomes. They manage formularies, prior authorization for medications, specialty pharmacy channels, manufacturer rebate negotiations, and medication adherence programs. Specialty drugs account for over 50% of drug spend despite representing less than 2% of prescriptions.

Care Management and Population Health organisations focus on longitudinal outcomes — risk stratification, chronic disease management, transitions of care, readmission prevention, and social determinants of health intervention. As value-based care models expand, these functions shift from supporting roles to strategic differentiators.

Specialty and Post-Acute Services extend care beyond the acute hospital episode — rehabilitation facilities, skilled nursing facilities (SNFs), home health agencies, hospice providers, and complex disease management programs. These settings manage the highest-cost, highest-risk patients and generate significant administrative burden.

Enterprise Enablers — technology platforms, analytics, compliance, finance, and GCC operations — provide the infrastructure through which the clinical domains function. They are increasingly the source of competitive differentiation as automation and AI transform what is possible in healthcare operations.

1.2 The Revenue Cycle as the Financial Nervous System

The revenue cycle is the connective tissue between clinical care and financial sustainability. It translates every patient encounter into a payment — or fails to. Understanding the revenue cycle architecture is prerequisite to understanding the entire healthcare operational landscape.

The cycle begins before the patient arrives and ends when the account is fully resolved:

Pre-service encompasses scheduling, pre-registration, insurance eligibility verification, prior authorization, and financial counseling. Front-end failures here are the largest source of preventable denials — 44% of all denials originate at the front end, with 24% tied specifically to registration and eligibility errors.

Point of service includes registration confirmation, consent documentation, co-pay collection, and real-time benefit estimation. Organizations that collect at point of service recover 2–3× more than those relying on post-service patient billing.

Mid-cycle covers clinical documentation, charge capture, coding (ICD-10-CM, CPT/HCPCS, DRG, APC), and claim preparation. Coding accuracy directly determines reimbursement accuracy — a single missed specificity element (laterality, chronicity, complication) can shift a DRG assignment by thousands of dollars.

Post-service manages claim submission, payer adjudication response, remittance processing, denial management, appeals, patient billing, payment plans, and collections. The average US provider takes 45–60 days to collect; best-in-class organizations achieve 30–35 days in AR.

Strategic statistics that define the challenge:

  • Average denial rate: 12% (Optum 2024)
  • Front-end denial contribution: 44%
  • Potentially avoidable denials: 84%
  • Net collection yield below 93%: reported by nearly half of provider finance leaders
  • Cost to collect: 3–5% of net patient revenue
  • Revenue leakage from denials and underpayments: 10–15% of revenue at poorly managed organizations

1.3 The Payer Operating Architecture

Payers operate a complex claim-processing machine that must simultaneously satisfy providers, members, regulators, and shareholders. The payer operating architecture has six core engines:

Enrollment and eligibility — managing member enrollment, life-event changes, COBRA processing, dependent verification, and real-time eligibility feeds to providers. Data quality failures here cascade into every downstream process.

Claims adjudication — the automated determination engine that evaluates eligibility, benefits, network status, prior authorization linkage, coding validity, medical necessity, coordination of benefits, and contract pricing to produce a pay, deny, adjust, or pend decision. Auto-adjudication rates of 85–95% are industry targets; below 80% indicates upstream data quality or rules gaps.

Utilization management — clinical review of services for medical necessity, including prior authorization, concurrent review of inpatient stays, retrospective review, and appeals. UM is simultaneously a cost-control mechanism and a provider friction point — the American Medical Association's 2023 survey found that 93% of physicians report that prior authorization delays necessary care.

Provider network management — recruitment, credentialing, contracting, reimbursement rate administration, network adequacy compliance, and provider data management. Provider data quality is one of the most chronic operational failures in healthcare — outdated provider directories generate millions of misdirected claims annually.

Payment integrity — identification of overpayments, duplicate payments, fraud, waste, and abuse through pre-payment editing, post-payment audit, and data analytics. The federal government estimates healthcare fraud costs $60–90 billion annually.

Member experience — call center operations, grievances and appeals, member portal management, care gap outreach, and chronic disease support. CAHPS (Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems) scores are published publicly and tied to Medicare Advantage Star ratings, making member experience directly connected to payer revenue.

1.4 The GCC Opportunity in Healthcare

Global Capability Centres in healthcare are evolving from transactional support centres into strategic capability hubs. The India GCC market was estimated at USD 69.85 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach USD 130.50 billion by 2033. Healthcare is among the fastest-growing GCC sectors, driven by the RCM outsourcing market, clinical informatics demand, and payer operations scale.

Why healthcare operations are GCC-ready:

Healthcare administrative operations are characterised by high volume, well-defined rules, structured data, and significant repetition — exactly the profile that makes work executable at scale from a GCC. Eligibility verification, claim editing, prior authorization intake, coding, payment posting, denial categorisation, and analytics reporting all meet this profile.

The GCC maturity spectrum in healthcare:

At the entry level, GCCs perform transactional processing — data entry, status checking, basic eligibility, and report generation. At the intermediate level, they manage operational workflows — coding centres, denial management teams, authorization intake, and analytics factories. At the advanced level, they own capability platforms — AI model development, process automation engineering, data engineering, and clinical informatics that serve multiple clients or hospital systems from a single GCC hub.

PwC estimates a potential GenAI economic impact of $23.5 billion per year across the GCC by 2030, with healthcare as a primary contributor given its data intensity, administrative burden, and need for scalable analytical capability.

1.5 The Digital Transformation Imperative

Healthcare's digital transformation is not optional. It is the mechanism through which the industry must resolve the cost-quality-access trilemma that has defined American healthcare for decades.

The four convergent forces:

AI and automation are reshaping what is possible. Natural language processing converts clinical notes to structured data. Robotic process automation handles payer portal navigation, claim status checking, and payment posting. Machine learning identifies denial risk before claims are submitted. Generative AI drafts appeal letters, member communications, and clinical documentation queries. These tools exist and are being deployed today by organisations willing to invest in the infrastructure they require.

Value-based care is shifting financial risk from payers to providers. ACOs, capitated arrangements, shared savings programs, and bundled payments reward outcomes rather than volume. Managing population risk requires data infrastructure, care management capability, and analytics sophistication that traditional fee-for-service operating models never demanded.

Interoperability mandates — driven by the 21st Century Cures Act, CMS Interoperability Rule, and HL7 FHIR standards — are creating the technical foundation for data exchange across the ecosystem. For the first time, patient data can flow between providers, payers, and patients with standardised formats and APIs.

Workforce transformation is unavoidable. Healthcare faces a chronic shortage of billing specialists, coders, and administrative professionals. Automation is not displacing this workforce — it is the only realistic path to maintaining throughput as the workforce shrinks and ages.

Chapter 2: Revenue Cycle Management — End-to-End Operating Model

Executive Summary

Revenue Cycle Management is the financial nervous system of every healthcare organisation. It converts clinical encounters into revenue, connects provider operations to payer payment engines, and creates the patient financial experience that increasingly drives satisfaction scores and loyalty. A mature RCM operating model is not merely a billing department — it is an integrated intelligence and operations system spanning patient access, clinical documentation, coding, claims management, denial prevention, patient collections, and analytics. This chapter provides the complete RCM architecture: the value chain, the organisational model, the technology stack, the key performance indicators, and the GCC delivery opportunities at each stage.

2.1 The RCM Value Chain — Eleven Stages

The RCM value chain connects the clinical encounter to final payment collection through eleven interdependent stages. Each stage has distinct process requirements, failure modes, measurement metrics, and GCC delivery potential.

Stage 1: Scheduling and Capacity Management

Scheduling is the entry point of the revenue cycle — where demand meets supply and where the patient financial journey begins. Effective scheduling includes appointment type accuracy (ensuring the encounter is scheduled under the correct clinical service code), provider availability management, referral capture, insurance collection prompting, and pre-registration initiation. Poor scheduling practices — incorrect appointment types, missing referral information, incorrect provider assignment — cascade as defects through every downstream stage.

GCC opportunity: Scheduling support, appointment reminder management, no-show outreach, and scheduling analytics are high-volume, rules-driven functions well-suited to GCC delivery.

Stage 2: Pre-Registration and Demographics

Pre-registration captures and validates the patient's demographic data, insurance information, guarantor details, and consent documentation before the date of service. The quality of pre-registration data is the single most important determinant of clean claim rate. A patient whose insurance ID, date of birth, or subscriber relationship is incorrectly captured will generate an eligibility denial that could have been prevented with 60 seconds of validation.

Critical data elements: Legal name (matching insurance card), date of birth, address, subscriber ID, group number, relationship to subscriber, secondary insurance details, and employer information.

Stage 3: Insurance Eligibility Verification

Eligibility verification confirms that the patient has active insurance coverage for the type of service to be rendered, on the date of service, with the identified provider. Real-time eligibility verification — using 270/271 electronic data interchange transactions or direct payer portal APIs — is the standard of care. Manual verification through payer phone calls or portals adds cost and delay and is insufficient at scale.

Best-practice eligibility verification occurs at three touchpoints: at scheduling (to confirm insurance before committing the appointment), 48–72 hours before the visit (to catch changes in coverage), and at point of service (to confirm the information captured matches the card in hand).

Key verification elements: Coverage active status, plan name and type, deductible and accumulator status, copay and coinsurance amounts, out-of-pocket maximum, network status of the rendering provider, and benefits specific to the service type (e.g., mental health, physical therapy, specialist visit limits).

AI opportunity: AI-powered eligibility engines that proactively flag patients with high denial risk based on coverage gaps, plan switching, or coverage type mismatches are demonstrating 30–40% reductions in eligibility-related denials.

Stage 4: Prior Authorisation

Prior authorisation (PA) is required by payers before certain services, procedures, imaging studies, and medications are provided. The PA requirement is payer-specific, plan-specific, and service-specific — creating an enormous operational complexity for providers who serve hundreds of payer plans simultaneously.

The PA workflow involves identifying which services require authorisation, submitting the request with clinical documentation demonstrating medical necessity, tracking the request status, obtaining and documenting the authorisation number, and ensuring the authorised service matches the service rendered.

PA failures — missing authorisations, expired authorisations, authorisations for incorrect service codes — are responsible for a significant proportion of clinical denials. The CMS estimate is that prior auth requirements cost providers $3.1 billion annually in administrative expense.

Automation opportunity: Gold Carding programs (exempting high-performing providers from PA requirements for specific services), AI-assisted PA submission with automated clinical criteria matching, and payer API integration for real-time PA status checking are the leading automation approaches.

Stage 5: Charge Capture

Charge capture translates clinical activity into billable charges. It is the bridge between clinical documentation and the revenue cycle. Incomplete, inaccurate, or delayed charge capture results in missed revenue that is often unrecoverable — unlike denial recovery, missed charges typically cannot be identified without retrospective clinical documentation review.

Common charge capture failure modes: Missed ancillary charges (supplies, medications, room charges), documentation-to-charge misalignment (service documented but not billed), charge entry delays that push claims past payer filing deadlines, and incorrect charge master item codes.

Revenue integrity controls: Charge Description Master (CDM) maintenance, charge reconciliation reporting (comparing OR logs to billed charges, ED visit documentation to charge capture), provider documentation completeness monitoring, and daily charge lag analysis.

Stage 6: Medical Coding

Medical coding is the translation of clinical documentation — physician notes, operative reports, discharge summaries, lab results — into standardised diagnosis codes (ICD-10-CM), procedure codes (CPT, HCPCS), and modifier codes that convey clinical specificity to payers. Coding accuracy determines payment accuracy: undercoding results in revenue loss; overcoding creates compliance risk.

ICD-10-CM classifies diseases, conditions, and reasons for encounter across 70,000+ codes. CPT codes (Current Procedural Terminology) describe physician services and procedures. HCPCS codes extend CPT for supplies, durable medical equipment, and services not covered by CPT. DRG codes (Diagnosis-Related Groups) determine inpatient hospital payment under Medicare.

Clinical Documentation Improvement (CDI) is the proactive process of engaging physicians to ensure clinical documentation supports the specificity of coding required for accurate reimbursement. CDI specialists review documentation concurrently (while the patient is still admitted) and retrospectively (after discharge) to identify documentation gaps that would result in undercoded diagnoses or procedures.

AI in coding: NLP-powered computer-assisted coding tools analyse clinical documentation and suggest appropriate ICD-10 and CPT codes, reducing coder time per chart and improving coding accuracy. Autonomous coding tools — that generate codes without coder review for clean, straightforward cases — are achieving 40–60% automation rates in appropriate encounter types.

Stage 7: Claim Preparation and Scrubbing

Before a claim is submitted to a payer, it passes through a claim scrubber — a rules engine that validates the claim against thousands of payer-specific and industry-standard editing rules. Claim edits check for code combinations that are never payable together (Correct Coding Initiative edits), codes that require modifiers to indicate specific circumstances, procedure codes that require specific diagnosis codes for coverage, and administrative requirements such as National Provider Identifier (NPI) and referring provider information.

Claims that fail scrubbing are returned to the revenue cycle for correction before submission — preserving the first-pass submission opportunity. Claims submitted without scrubbing generate rejections and denials that require expensive rework.

Stage 8: Claim Submission and Clearinghouse Processing

Clean claims are submitted electronically to payers via clearinghouses — intermediaries that translate claims from provider formats to payer-specific formats, perform additional validation, and route claims to the correct payer destination. Major healthcare clearinghouses include Availity, Change Healthcare (Optum), Waystar, and Apex EDI.

The 2024 Change Healthcare cyberattack — which disrupted claims processing for the majority of US healthcare providers for weeks — demonstrated the catastrophic operational risk of single-clearinghouse dependency. Provider organisations with multi-clearinghouse connectivity and backup submission pathways recovered significantly faster.

Stage 9: Remittance Processing and Payment Posting

When a payer processes a claim, it generates an Electronic Remittance Advice (ERA) — the 835 transaction — that itemises the payment, adjustments, and any denial reasons for each claim line. Accurate and timely remittance posting is essential for identifying underpayments, tracking denial trends, and maintaining accurate AR.

Contractual adjustment posting reconciles the billed charge against the payer-contracted rate. Balance billing rules determine what portion (if any) can be billed to the patient. Secondary billing routes the patient's secondary insurance balance for coordination of benefits processing.

Stage 10: Denial Management and Appeals

Denial management is the most analytically intensive and strategically consequential stage of the revenue cycle. Denials represent recovered revenue opportunity — but only if they are appealed timely, with appropriate supporting documentation, through the correct appeal pathway.

Effective denial management is covered in depth in Chapter 5. The key framework elements are denial taxonomy (classifying by root cause, not just denial code), prevention analytics (identifying upstream process failures), appeal workflow management (tracking appeal status, deadline, and outcome), and recovery rate reporting (measuring the percentage of denied value that is ultimately recovered).

Stage 11: Patient Financial Services and Collections

Patient financial services includes financial counselling, charity care screening, payment plan establishment, patient statement generation, and self-pay collection. As patient financial responsibility grows — driven by high-deductible health plans that have shifted more cost to patients — patient collections have become a larger and more complex component of the revenue cycle.

Patient financial experience is increasingly a competitive differentiator. Price transparency, upfront cost estimates, and flexible payment options are moving from nice-to-have features to baseline patient expectations.

2.2 RCM Organisational Design

The most effective RCM organisations are structured to align accountability with the value chain stages where revenue is created and protected.

Front Office (Patient-Facing):

  • Scheduling
  • Pre-registration and registration
  • Financial counselling and charity care screening
  • Point-of-service collections
  • Patient access analytics

Middle Office (Clinical Interface):

  • CDI and clinical documentation
  • Medical coding
  • Charge capture and reconciliation
  • Utilisation review and case management
  • Prior authorisation

Back Office (Claims and Finance):

  • Claim preparation and scrubbing
  • Claim submission and clearinghouse management
  • Remittance processing and payment posting
  • Denial management and appeals
  • Patient billing and collections
  • Revenue integrity and analytics

GCC Delivery Model:

High-volume, rules-based, analytically intensive functions in all three offices are appropriate for GCC delivery — particularly eligibility verification, coding, CDI support, denial analytics, remittance posting, and collections analytics. Clinical functions requiring US-licensed professionals (physician advisors for medical necessity reviews, licensed nurses for utilisation management) must remain in the US operating environment.

2.3 RCM Technology Stack

The modern RCM technology architecture spans five layers.

Layer 1: Core Clinical Systems. EHR (Epic, Oracle Health, Meditech), practice management system (scheduling, registration, billing). The EHR is the source of clinical documentation and the origination point for charges and orders.

Layer 2: Revenue Cycle Platform. Claim scrubbing engine, denials management platform, AR workflow management, remittance processing, and patient billing. Leading platforms include nThrive, Waystar, R1 RCM, and Epic's native revenue cycle modules.

Layer 3: Analytics and Intelligence. Business intelligence reporting (Power BI, Tableau), RCM analytics platforms (Strata, Axiom), AI-powered denial prediction, coding AI (3M, Optum360, Nuance), and clinical documentation AI (Nuance DAX, Abridge, Suki).

Layer 4: Automation and Integration. RPA (UiPath, Automation Anywhere), EDI clearinghouse connectivity, payer API integration (FHIR-based prior auth APIs mandated by CMS 2024), and patient engagement platforms.

Layer 5: Governance and Compliance. HIPAA compliance monitoring, audit management, provider data management, and regulatory reporting.

2.4 RCM Key Performance Indicators

A mature RCM measurement framework tracks performance across five dimensions.

Access Quality

  • Pre-registration rate: % of visits with pre-registration completed before DOS
  • Eligibility verification rate: % of visits with real-time eligibility confirmation
  • Authorization capture rate: % of visits requiring PA with auth on file
  • Point-of-service collection rate: $ collected / $ collectible at POS

Claim Quality

  • Clean claim rate (first-pass): % of claims accepted without rejection or edit
  • Claim rejection rate: % of submitted claims rejected by clearinghouse or payer
  • Days to submit: median days from DOS to claim submission

AR Performance

  • Days in Accounts Receivable (AR): total AR / average daily charges
  • AR >90 days: % of total AR aged beyond 90 days
  • AR >180 days: % of total AR aged beyond 180 days
  • Cash collections as % of net patient service revenue

Denial Management

  • Denial rate (first pass): % of claims denied on first submission
  • Denial overturn rate: % of appealed denials successfully overturned
  • Net denial rate: % of claims denied after appeals exhausted
  • Cost per denial: administrative cost to work one denial

Patient Financial Services

  • Bad debt rate: % of gross revenue written off as uncollectable
  • Charity care rate: % of gross revenue granted as charity
  • Self-pay collection yield: $ collected / $ billed to self-pay patients

Chapter 3: Provider Domain — Clinical Operations & Governance

Executive Summary

Provider organisations — hospitals, health systems, physician groups, ambulatory surgery centres — are the care delivery engine of the healthcare system. Their digital and operational transformation is driven by four simultaneous forces: margin compression demanding cost efficiency, payer friction driving administrative investment, workforce shortage creating automation urgency, and patient expectations requiring consumer-grade experience. This chapter examines the seven pillars of clinical governance, patient flow optimisation, and the GCC opportunities that are reshaping provider operations.

3.1 Seven Pillars of Clinical Governance

Clinical governance is the system by which healthcare organisations are accountable for continuously improving the quality of their services and safeguarding high standards of care. It comprises seven interdependent pillars that must function together to create a high-performing provider organisation.

Pillar 1: Clinical Effectiveness. Evidence-based protocols, clinical pathway adherence, outcome measurement, and benchmarking against national quality standards. Organisations that adopt and monitor clinical pathways consistently achieve 15–25% reductions in unnecessary variation, length of stay, and complications.

Pillar 2: Patient Safety. Adverse event reporting, near-miss analysis, medication safety programs, surgical safety checklists, infection control, and the culture of psychological safety that enables clinicians to report concerns without fear of blame. The Institute for Healthcare Improvement's frameworks for safety culture measurement are the industry standard.

Pillar 3: Patient Experience. HCAHPS (Hospital Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems) performance, patient engagement, communication quality, care coordination, and discharge experience. Patient experience scores increasingly affect value-based payment and CMS star ratings.

Pillar 4: Workforce Development. Clinical competency frameworks, continuing medical education, nursing certification programs, and the succession planning that ensures leadership continuity in high-turnover clinical environments.

Pillar 5: Risk Management. Clinical liability identification, incident investigation, malpractice exposure management, and the documentation practices that protect both patients and organisations.

Pillar 6: Information Governance. Clinical data quality, EHR documentation standards, interoperability compliance, and the data stewardship practices that determine whether the organisation's information assets can be used for quality improvement, research, and analytics.

Pillar 7: Clinical Audit and Improvement. The systematic review of clinical practice against defined standards, with structured improvement cycles that close the loop between assessment and action.

3.2 Patient Flow Optimisation

Patient flow — the movement of patients through the healthcare system from initial access through discharge and follow-up — is one of the highest-value operational improvement domains in provider organisations. Flow inefficiencies manifest as ED overcrowding, surgical case delays, excessive length of stay, and care transitions that expose patients to readmission risk.

Emergency Department flow: ED throughput is measured by Door-to-Provider time (target <30 minutes for ESI-2 patients), Left Without Being Seen rate (<2%), and ED Length of Stay. AI-powered triage decision support tools, real-time patient tracking systems, and predictive volume modelling are the leading technology interventions.

Inpatient flow: The core inpatient flow metric is Average Length of Stay (ALOS) versus Geometric Mean Length of Stay (GMLOS) — the CMS-expected length of stay for each DRG. Organisations with ALOS significantly exceeding GMLOS are leaving case mix-adjusted revenue unrealised and creating bed capacity constraints. Care management, utilisation review, and discharge planning functions are the primary levers.

Surgical throughput: Surgical schedule block utilisation, first-case on-time starts, turnover time between cases, and case cancellation rates are the operational metrics that determine OR efficiency. AI-powered surgical scheduling tools that optimise block assignments, predict case duration, and flag cancellation risk are demonstrating 15–20% improvements in OR utilisation.

3.3 Provider Credentialing and Enrollment

Provider credentialing is the process by which healthcare organisations verify that clinicians have the education, training, licensure, and competency required to deliver safe care. Provider enrollment is the related process of registering providers with payers to establish billing rights.

Credentialing failures — privileging a provider who does not meet competency standards, failing to detect a licence suspension, or missing a malpractice exclusion — create profound patient safety and liability risk. Enrollment failures — missed enrollment deadlines, incorrect taxonomy codes, invalid billing identifiers — create revenue loss through claim denials and payment delays.

Credentialing data elements: Medical school and residency verification, board certification, state licensure (all active states), DEA registration, malpractice history (National Practitioner Data Bank query), exclusion screening (OIG, SAM.gov), hospital privileges, and peer references.

GCC opportunity: Credentialing primary source verification, enrollment application processing, CAQH profile maintenance, payer portal management, and re-credentialing monitoring are high-volume, rules-based functions with significant GCC delivery potential.

3.4 Ambulatory Surgery Center (ASC) and Hospital at Home Models

Ambulatory Surgery Centers are free-standing outpatient surgical facilities that perform procedures that do not require hospital admission. ASCs represent the most significant site-of-care shift in surgical delivery — driven by payer pressure to move appropriate cases from hospital outpatient to ASC settings (at 40–60% lower reimbursement) and by patient preference for convenience.

Hospital at Home programs — accelerated by the CMS Acute Hospital Care at Home waiver during COVID-19 — provide hospital-level care in the patient's home for medically appropriate admissions. Hospital at Home requires a sophisticated remote monitoring, nursing visit, and physician oversight infrastructure that is increasingly supported by AI-powered monitoring platforms and GCC-delivered clinical support teams.

Chapter 4: Payer Domain — Claims Administration & Risk Adjustment

Executive Summary

Payers sit at the financial centre of the healthcare system — managing the risk, benefits, and payments for hundreds of millions of Americans across commercial, Medicare Advantage, and Medicaid managed care programs. Their operational complexity is enormous: every plan has unique benefit designs, network configurations, authorisation requirements, and medical policies. Their financial stakes are high: a 1% improvement in medical cost management at a large payer translates to hundreds of millions of dollars. This chapter examines the payer operating model, the CMS-HCC V28 risk adjustment transition, the claims adjudication architecture, and the GCC opportunities across payer operations.

4.1 The Payer Operating Model

Payer organisations operate across five interconnected functional domains.

Member services and enrollment. Managing the member lifecycle from application and eligibility determination through ongoing benefits administration, member communications, and disenrollment. Member enrollment accuracy is foundational to all downstream operations — eligibility errors propagate through claims adjudication, prior authorisation, and member experience.

Network management. Recruiting, contracting, credentialing, and managing the provider network that serves plan members. Network adequacy — having sufficient providers within geographic access standards for each specialty — is a regulatory requirement and a competitive differentiator. Network management GCCs own provider data management, contract loading, credentialing support, and directory accuracy maintenance.

Claims administration. The full claims lifecycle from intake through adjudication, payment, and appeals. Claims operations are the highest-volume, most rules-intensive, and most automation-receptive function in payer organisations. The claims adjudication architecture is detailed in Section 4.3.

Medical management. Utilisation management (prior authorisation, concurrent review, discharge planning), case management, disease management, and care management programs that collectively determine how efficiently plan members use healthcare services. Medical management is the primary lever for controlling the Medical Loss Ratio.

Analytics and actuarial. Risk adjustment, premium setting, reserve management, quality reporting (HEDIS, STAR), and population health analytics. Analytics functions are increasingly the strategic differentiator between payers — organisations with superior predictive models for risk stratification, denials management, and fraud detection consistently outperform peers on both MLR and member satisfaction.

4.2 CMS-HCC V28: The Risk Adjustment Revolution

The CMS Hierarchical Condition Category (HCC) risk adjustment model is used to adjust Medicare Advantage capitation payments based on the expected health needs of each plan's enrolled population. A plan with sicker members (higher risk scores) receives higher capitation payments; a plan with healthier members receives lower payments.

V28 transition. CMS implemented HCC V28 in 2024, replacing the V24 model that had been in use since 2015. V28 makes significant changes to the condition hierarchy, adds new condition categories, removes conditions previously used for risk adjustment, and changes the relative weights of conditions. The transition to V28 is expected to reduce risk scores for most Medicare Advantage plans, creating significant pressure on plan economics.

Risk adjustment accuracy. Plans that accurately identify and document the diagnoses of their enrolled population receive appropriate risk-adjusted payments. Plans that under-document — fail to capture chronic conditions that have been assessed and managed during the plan year — receive less than their actuarially appropriate payment. This creates a financial incentive for robust clinical documentation, retrospective chart review, and member outreach to capture diagnosed conditions.

GCC risk adjustment operations. Risk adjustment coding, chart retrieval, retrospective coding review, provider documentation outreach, and RAF (Risk Adjustment Factor) analytics are GCC-deliverable functions. Major payer GCCs in India are significant contributors to their parent organisations' risk adjustment programs.

4.3 Claims Adjudication Architecture

Claims adjudication is the core payer process that converts submitted claims into payment decisions. The architecture involves multiple sequential stages, each performing specific validations and applying specific business rules.

Intake and syntax validation. The first stage confirms that the claim file is properly formatted (ANSI X12 837 standard), complete, and non-duplicative. Syntax errors trigger rejections before adjudication begins.

Eligibility and enrollment verification. The claim is matched against the member enrollment database to confirm active coverage on the date of service. Coverage type, plan, and effective date are verified.

Provider verification. The billing and rendering provider NPIs are validated against the provider directory, network status is determined, and taxonomy codes are verified against the billed services.

Benefit and policy application. The claim is evaluated against the member's specific benefit plan — copay, coinsurance, deductible, coverage limits, exclusions, and prior authorisation requirements. Medical policy is applied to determine coverage for specific services.

Pricing and payment calculation. For in-network providers, contracted rates are applied. For out-of-network providers, applicable reimbursement rules (UCR rates, reference-based pricing, balance billing protections) determine payment.

Coordination of benefits. For members with multiple payers, COB rules determine primary versus secondary payment responsibility.

Decisioning and output. The claim receives a disposition: pay, deny, pend, or adjust. The ERA (835 transaction) is generated with payment details and denial reason codes.

4.4 Medical Loss Ratio Management

The Medical Loss Ratio (MLR) is the percentage of premium revenue that a health insurer spends on medical claims and quality improvement activities. The ACA mandates minimum MLR of 80% for individual and small group markets and 85% for large group markets. For Medicare Advantage plans, CMS monitors MLR as a financial sustainability indicator.

Managing MLR requires simultaneous optimisation of the numerator (medical costs) and denominator (premium revenue). Medical cost management includes utilisation management, care management, network strategy, and payment integrity. Revenue management includes accurate risk adjustment, appropriate premium setting, and member mix management.

Payment integrity — detecting and recovering fraudulent, wasteful, and erroneous payments — is a critical MLR management tool. US healthcare fraud, waste, and abuse (FWA) is estimated to cost $300+ billion annually. Payer payment integrity programs using AI, predictive analytics, and clinical review are recovering billions annually in overpayments.

Chapter 5: Denial Management & Prevention — The Revenue Recovery Playbook

Executive Summary

Denial management is the most strategically consequential function in the revenue cycle. Denials represent 10–15% of gross charges at the average US health system — a revenue leakage that directly compresses operating margins. The Optum 2024 Denials Index confirmed that 84% of denials are potentially avoidable. This means denial management done right is not primarily a back-end recovery function: it is a prevention architecture that addresses the root causes of denials upstream, before claims are submitted. This chapter provides the complete denial management playbook.

5.1 Denial Taxonomy

A mature denial management system begins with a clear, operational taxonomy. Without consistent classification, denial data cannot drive prevention actions. The industry-standard taxonomy separates denials into five major categories.

Eligibility and Coverage Denials. The patient was not covered on the date of service, the service was not covered under the plan, or the coverage information captured at registration was inaccurate. Prevention: real-time eligibility verification at multiple touchpoints.

Authorization and Pre-Certification Denials. A required prior authorisation was not obtained, was obtained for a different service, or was obtained from a different payer. Prevention: payer-specific auth requirement libraries embedded in scheduling and access workflows.

Coding and Modifier Denials. Diagnosis or procedure codes were incorrect, mutually exclusive, unbundled inappropriately, or missing required modifiers. Prevention: CDI programs, coder education, and AI-powered coding review.

Documentation and Medical Necessity Denials. The clinical documentation does not support the level of service billed, the diagnosis does not support the procedure, or medical necessity criteria were not met. Prevention: concurrent CDI, physician advisor review, and medical necessity criteria integration in utilisation management.

Timely Filing and Administrative Denials. The claim was submitted after the payer's filing deadline, or an administrative requirement (NPI, referring provider, taxonomy code) was missing. Prevention: claim submission tracking and workflow management.

5.2 The Prevention Architecture

The HFMA position paper on denial prevention establishes that prevention requires connecting denial codes to operational process origins — not just tracking denial codes in isolation. The prevention architecture must span all three revenue cycle offices.

Front-end prevention: Real-time eligibility verification at scheduling and day-before-service. Automated auth requirement lookup at order entry. Pre-registration completeness scoring with visit-day hold for incomplete demographics. Staff training on most common front-end failure patterns.

Mid-cycle prevention: CDI concurrent query workflow for complex inpatient cases. Coding quality audits with physician education feedback loops. Charge capture reconciliation against OR logs and procedure documentation. Pre-claim edit engine with payer-specific edit libraries.

Back-end prevention: Root-cause analysis of every denial class exceeding $50K monthly impact. Prevention action tracking with accountability owners. Payer escalation for systemic denial patterns. Monthly denial prevention governance meeting with VP-level ownership.

5.3 Denial Workflow Management

The denial workflow must be structured to maximise recovery while generating the data needed for prevention.

Step 1 — Classification: Assign the denial to a category (taxonomy above) and subcategory (specific denial code and payer-specific reason).

Step 2 — Financial triage: Calculate the dollar value and age of the denial. Prioritise appeals by financial impact and appeal deadline proximity.

Step 3 — Root-cause analysis: Identify the specific process failure that caused the denial. Was it a missing auth? An incorrect ICD code? An expired insurance card? Document the root cause at the workflow system level.

Step 4 — Appeal decision: Determine whether the denial is appealable, correctable and resubmittable, or appropriate for write-off. Do not write off without root-cause documentation.

Step 5 — Appeal execution: Prepare the appeal with all supporting documentation — medical records, authorisation numbers, clinical criteria justification. Submit within the payer's appeal window (typically 60–180 days from denial date).

Step 6 — Outcome tracking: Track appeal resolution rates, denial overturn rates by category and payer, and time to resolution.

Step 7 — Prevention feedback: Route root-cause data to the relevant upstream process owner — scheduling, registration, coding, CDI, utilisation management — for workflow improvement.

5.4 AI in Denial Prevention and Management

AI is transforming denial management across three application categories.

Predictive denial prevention. Machine learning models trained on historical claim and denial data can predict, at the point of claim creation, which claims have elevated denial risk. Claims flagged as high-risk are routed for human review before submission, enabling intervention before the claim reaches the payer. Leading implementations report 25–40% reductions in preventable denials.

Automated appeal generation. NLP tools can draft payer-specific appeal letters from clinical documentation, authorisation records, and denial reason codes — reducing appeal preparation time from 45 minutes to under 5 minutes per appeal and increasing appeal volume without additional staffing.

Pattern recognition and payer intelligence. AI analytics platforms that continuously monitor denial patterns by payer, service type, provider, and time period identify emerging payer policy changes and systemic denial spikes before they reach significant dollar impact.

Chapter 6: Patient Eligibility Verification & Prior Authorisation

Executive Summary

Eligibility and prior authorisation failures account for nearly a third of all claim denials — and nearly all of them are preventable. Real-time eligibility verification and efficient authorisation management are the two highest-ROI investments a revenue cycle organisation can make. This chapter provides the operating model, technology requirements, and AI opportunity for both functions.

6.1 Eligibility Verification — Operating Model

Eligibility verification must occur at three touchpoints: at scheduling (to confirm insurance before committing the appointment), 48–72 hours before service (to catch coverage changes), and at point of service (to confirm card and ID match).

What to verify: Active member status, plan name and effective dates, deductible and accumulator status (YTD amounts), copay and coinsurance for the service type, network status of the rendering provider, service-specific benefit limits (e.g., visit limits for physical therapy, mental health), and referral requirements.

Technology: The 270/271 EDI transaction is the industry standard for electronic eligibility inquiry and response. Real-time payer API integration (increasingly FHIR-based under CMS mandates) provides faster and more granular eligibility data than batch 270/271 transactions. Clearinghouse-based eligibility tools aggregate multi-payer eligibility checking through a single portal.

AI opportunity: Predictive eligibility risk scoring — identifying patients with historically unstable coverage, frequent plan switching, or high probability of coverage lapse — enables proactive outreach before the visit to confirm or update coverage.

6.2 Prior Authorisation — The Operational Challenge

Prior authorisation requires providers to obtain payer approval before delivering specific services. The operational challenge is the variability of PA requirements across payers, plans, and services — a provider group working with 200 payer contracts may face 1,000+ unique authorisation requirement combinations.

PA workflow:

  1. Identify whether the ordered service requires PA (payer-specific, plan-specific)
  2. Verify member eligibility and plan details
  3. Gather clinical documentation supporting medical necessity
  4. Submit PA request (fax, portal, phone, or API)
  5. Track PA status and follow up on pending requests
  6. Document the authorisation number in the scheduling and billing systems
  7. Verify that the authorised service matches the service to be rendered
  8. Re-authorise when service dates or service scope changes

Common PA failure modes: Missing PA (service not identified as requiring PA), expired PA (service delivered after authorisation period ended), PA for wrong service or wrong CPT code, PA obtained from wrong plan when COB primary has changed.

6.3 The CMS 2024 Prior Auth Rule

The CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule (CMS-0057-F), effective for Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, and CHIP managed care plans by January 2026, mandates:

  • PA decisions within 72 hours for urgent/expedited requests
  • PA decisions within 7 calendar days for standard requests
  • Plain-language denial explanations
  • 90-day PA validity for patients with chronic conditions
  • FHIR-based APIs for electronic PA submission and status checking
  • Gold carding exemptions for high-performing providers

Provider impact: FHIR-based PA APIs will transform the PA workflow from manual portal/fax interactions to automated, real-time API transactions — dramatically reducing administrative burden for high-volume PA requesters.

6.4 Gold Carding and Exemption Programs

Gold carding programs exempt providers who demonstrate consistent prior authorisation approval rates (typically >90%) for specific services from PA requirements for those services. Several states have enacted gold card legislation for commercial insurers. CMS has encouraged gold carding in Medicare Advantage.

Gold carding is the most impactful structural solution to prior authorisation burden — but requires payers to invest in performance tracking infrastructure and providers to maintain the documentation quality that supports their approval history.

Chapter 7: Medical Coding & Clinical Documentation Improvement

Executive Summary

Medical coding and Clinical Documentation Improvement (CDI) are the intellectual core of the revenue cycle. They translate clinical complexity into financial language — ICD-10 codes that define diagnoses, CPT codes that define procedures, and DRGs that determine inpatient payment. Coding accuracy is simultaneously a revenue integrity issue (undercoding leaves money on the table), a compliance issue (overcoding creates FWA risk), and a quality measurement issue (diagnosis codes drive quality metrics, risk scores, and population health analytics). This chapter provides the complete coding and CDI operating model.

7.1 The Coding Ecosystem

ICD-10-CM (International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision, Clinical Modification) classifies diseases, injuries, symptoms, and reasons for encounter. With 70,000+ codes organised in a hierarchical structure, ICD-10-CM provides the diagnostic specificity that drives DRG assignment, risk adjustment, quality reporting, and population health analytics.

CPT (Current Procedural Terminology) describes physician and outpatient facility services and procedures. CPT codes drive professional fee billing and are required for all Part B claims. Modifier codes provide additional specificity — laterality, procedural context, session sequence — that affects both payment and compliance.

HCPCS (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System) Level II codes describe supplies, durable medical equipment, drugs, and services not covered by CPT — critical for outpatient pharmacy billing, DME, ambulance services, and prosthetics.

DRG (Diagnosis-Related Group) codes determine Medicare inpatient payment. Each MS-DRG maps to a payment weight that multiplies the hospital's base rate to determine the episode payment. The principal diagnosis, secondary diagnoses (complication and comorbidity status), procedures, and discharge disposition all affect DRG assignment.

7.2 Clinical Documentation Improvement

CDI is the proactive process of engaging clinicians to ensure that medical documentation accurately reflects the patient's clinical condition, the services provided, and the complexity of care — enabling accurate and complete coding.

Concurrent CDI (inpatient): CDI specialists review medical records while the patient is admitted, identifying documentation gaps and issuing physician queries to clarify ambiguous, contradictory, or incomplete clinical information. Concurrent CDI has direct impact on DRG assignment and case mix index.

Retrospective CDI (post-discharge): Review of discharged records to identify coding opportunities missed during concurrent review. Less impactful for DRG optimisation (most payers only allow DRG changes on appeal) but valuable for coding quality measurement and physician education.

Ambulatory CDI (outpatient): A rapidly growing CDI application focused on HCC risk adjustment documentation in physician and specialty clinic settings. Ensures that chronic condition diagnoses assessed and managed during ambulatory encounters are documented with ICD-10 specificity required for risk adjustment.

7.3 The Physician Query Process

When documentation is ambiguous, conflicting, or insufficiently specific for accurate coding, CDI specialists and coders submit physician queries — formal questions seeking clarification.

Query standards: AHIMA and ACDIS have established guidelines for compliant physician queries. Queries must be non-leading (not suggesting a specific diagnosis), clinically supported (based on documented clinical indicators), and professionally formatted. Non-compliant queries create FWA risk.

Query response rate is a key CDI performance metric. Organisations with structured physician CDI engagement programs achieve query response rates of 85–95%. Those without structured programs typically see 40–60% response rates with slower turnaround times.

7.4 AI in Coding and CDI

Computer-Assisted Coding (CAC): NLP tools analyse clinical documentation and suggest ICD-10 and CPT codes. CAC tools increase coder throughput and reduce coding variation. The most advanced CAC platforms achieve 40–60% autonomous coding rates for straightforward encounter types (routine office visits, simple ED encounters) while routing complex cases to human coders.

CDI AI: AI tools that identify documentation gaps in real time — flagging patients who likely have additional diagnoses based on clinical indicators in labs, medications, and nursing notes — increase CDI query rates and decrease the manual chart review time required per case.

Autonomous CDI Queries: Some platforms now generate physician queries automatically based on AI-identified documentation gaps, dramatically increasing query volume without proportional CDI staffing increases.

7.5 ICD-10 Specificity: The Revenue Impact

ICD-10-CM coding specificity directly determines DRG assignment for inpatient claims and risk adjustment score for managed care populations. A physician who documents "heart failure" without specifying systolic versus diastolic, acute versus chronic, and left versus right ventricular involvement leaves significant revenue on the table — the HCC risk adjustment score for "systolic heart failure" is materially higher than for generic "heart failure," and the DRG payment for a more complex HF presentation is meaningfully higher than for a simple HF diagnosis.

High-specificity diagnosis categories with major revenue impact:

  • Heart failure (systolic vs diastolic; acute vs chronic; left vs right vs combined)
  • Sepsis (SIRS vs sepsis vs severe sepsis vs septic shock — major DRG payment differences)
  • Malnutrition (mild vs moderate vs severe — affects complication status)
  • Acute kidney injury (vs chronic kidney disease vs end-stage renal disease)
  • Encephalopathy (metabolic, toxic, anoxic — CC vs MCC status varies)
  • Pressure injuries (Stage 1–4 and unstageable — major CC/MCC implications)

CDI impact on case mix index: Organisations with mature CDI programs consistently report case mix index improvements of 0.08–0.15 over 2–3 year CDI program maturity periods. 7.6 Ambulatory Coding and Evaluation & Management

E/M (Evaluation and Management) coding for outpatient office visits underwent a fundamental restructuring with the 2021 AMA CPT code revisions, which replaced the four-element documentation-based E/M selection model with a two-element model: medical decision making (MDM) complexity OR total time.

MDM complexity is assessed across three elements: the number and complexity of problems addressed, the amount and complexity of data reviewed, and the risk of complications and morbidity. The intersection of these three elements — each scored as minimal, low, moderate, or high — determines the MDM complexity level (straightforward, low, moderate, high) and thus the E/M code (99213, 99214, 99215).

Time-based E/M coding: Providers who spend a defined total time (minimum threshold) on the face-to-face encounter plus all related pre- and post-encounter work on the date of service may select the E/M code based solely on time — a significant simplification from the previous documentation-intensive approach.

GCC coding quality applications: Retrospective coding audits, E/M level analysis (identifying systematic undercoding or overcoding patterns by physician), and coder productivity analytics are GCC-deliverable functions that support both revenue integrity and compliance objectives.

Chapter 8: Claims Adjudication — The Payer Engine

Executive Summary

Claims adjudication is the core payer process that determines whether healthcare claims are paid, denied, adjusted, or pended. It is simultaneously the highest-volume operation in payer organisations and the most consequential — converting benefit design, provider contracts, eligibility, and medical policy into payment decisions that affect provider cash flow, member financial experience, and payer financial performance. This chapter provides the complete adjudication operating model.

8.1 The Five-Stage Adjudication Lifecycle

Stage 1 — Intake and Validation. Claims received from providers (via clearinghouse or direct submission) are checked for format compliance (ANSI X12 837), completeness (required data elements), and non-duplication. Invalid claims generate rejections (returned without entering adjudication); duplicate claims are flagged for investigation.

Stage 2 — Eligibility and Benefit Determination. The claim is matched to the member enrollment record. Eligibility on the date of service, plan assignment, benefit design, deductible accumulator status, and cost-sharing obligations are determined. Claims for ineligible members generate eligibility denials.

Stage 3 — Medical Policy and Authorisation Review. The service is evaluated against the plan's medical policy — coverage criteria, quantity limits, age restrictions, and authorisation requirements. Claims lacking required authorisations, or where the service does not meet coverage criteria, are denied at this stage.

Stage 4 — Pricing and Contract Application. For contracted providers, the applicable fee schedule or alternative payment arrangement is applied to calculate allowed amounts. For non-contracted providers, applicable OON reimbursement rules are applied.

Stage 5 — Payment Calculation and Output. Member cost-sharing (deductible, copay, coinsurance) is calculated and applied. The claim is finalised with a pay, deny, adjust, or pend disposition. The ERA (835 transaction) is generated. Member EOB is generated. Provider payment is released.

8.2 Auto-Adjudication: The Efficiency Frontier

Auto-adjudication — the processing of claims through all five stages without human intervention — is the primary efficiency metric for payer claims operations. Best-in-class payers achieve 90–95% auto-adjudication rates. Organisations below 80% are significantly inefficient.

Auto-adjudication barriers: Complex clinical edits that require human judgment, missing provider contract data, incomplete benefit configuration, claims requiring coordination of benefits determination, and claims flagged for fraud review.

AI enhancement: Machine learning models that predict which claims flagged by edit engines are likely to be payable (reducing unnecessary human review) and which auto-adjudicated claims have high payment error probability (enabling targeted post-payment audit) are increasing both auto-adjudication rates and payment accuracy simultaneously.

8.3 Claims Editing and Edit Management

Claims editing is the automated application of business rules to claims before adjudication decisions are made. Editing engines contain thousands of rules organised into categories.

Correct Coding Initiative (CCI) edits: CMS-developed edits that prevent improper payment when codes that should be reported together are billed separately (unbundling), or when codes that are mutually exclusive are billed on the same claim.

Medical necessity edits: Diagnosis-to-procedure links that confirm the billed procedure is appropriate for the billed diagnosis according to clinical coverage criteria.

Duplicate claim edits: Logic that identifies claims that appear to duplicate a previously paid claim based on member, provider, date of service, and procedure code matching.

Payer-specific edits: Custom rules reflecting each payer's unique benefit design, network configuration, and medical policy.

8.4 Coordination of Benefits

When a member has coverage from multiple payers, COB rules determine primary versus secondary payment responsibility. The COB determination prevents double payment for a single service. COB failures — where both payers pay independently, or where neither payer pays because each believes the other is primary — are a significant source of payment error.

Birthday rule: The most common COB determination for dependents covered by both parents' employer plans: the plan of the parent whose birthday falls earliest in the calendar year is primary.

Medicare as secondary payer: Medicare has specific rules for when it pays as secondary to employer group health plans, liability insurance, workers' compensation, and other primary payers.

Chapter 9: Population Health & Value-Based Care

Executive Summary

Value-based care (VBC) represents the most significant structural transformation in US healthcare payment in a generation — shifting reimbursement from fee-for-service volume to outcome-based performance. ACO REACH, Medicare Advantage quality star programs, episode-based bundles, and the mandatory TEAM model are creating financial accountability for health outcomes that demands population health management infrastructure, risk stratification capabilities, and care management programs that did not exist in the fee-for-service era. This chapter provides the population health and VBC operating model.

9.1 The Value-Based Care Continuum

Value-based payment models exist on a continuum from fee-for-service with quality adjustments to full capitation with risk transfer.

Pay-for-Performance (P4P): Fee-for-service base with quality bonuses or penalties based on performance against defined metrics. Minimal risk transfer. Examples: Medicare Merit-based Incentive Payment System (MIPS), commercial quality incentive programs.

Shared Savings: Providers share in savings generated when actual costs are below a target, but are not responsible for losses if costs exceed target. Example: Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) Track 1.

Shared Savings with Downside Risk: Providers share both in savings and in losses. Example: MSSP Track 3, ACO REACH Basic.

Episode-Based Payments: A single payment covers all services associated with a defined clinical episode. Example: CMS Bundled Payments for Care Improvement (BPCI) Advanced, mandatory TEAM model for joint replacements.

Global Capitation: A fixed per-member-per-month payment covering all healthcare services for a defined population. Maximum risk transfer. Example: Medicare Advantage capitation, Medicaid managed care capitation.

9.2 ACO REACH: The New Accountability Model

ACO REACH (Realizing Equity, Access, and Community Health) replaced the Global and Professional Direct Contracting Model in 2023. ACO REACH expands value-based care to smaller and rural providers, introduces equity benchmarking, and emphasises social determinants of health integration.

PY2026 updates: Benchmark methodology refinements, health equity adjustment factor updates, and expanded geographic risk adjustment to account for community-level social risk factors. Organisations participating in ACO REACH must develop health equity plans and track equity metrics alongside financial performance.

9.3 Risk Stratification: The Foundation of Population Health

Risk stratification assigns patients to risk tiers based on their probability of future high-cost utilisation, readmission, or clinical deterioration. Effective stratification enables care management resources to be directed to the patients who will benefit most.

Stratification inputs: Claims data (diagnoses, utilisation history, pharmacy), EHR data (vital signs, labs, care gaps), socioeconomic data (social determinants, area deprivation index), patient-reported data (functional status, self-efficacy), and real-time clinical data (current admission, recent ED visit).

Stratification models: Traditional claims-based models (Johns Hopkins ACG, 3M CRG) are being supplemented by machine learning models that incorporate EHR and SDoH data and demonstrate significantly improved predictive accuracy for high-cost, high-risk patients.

Risk tiers:

  • Tier 1 (High risk): Complex medical and social needs; intensive case management, care coordination, home visits
  • Tier 2 (Rising risk): Multiple chronic conditions, recent hospitalisations; proactive outreach, disease management
  • Tier 3 (Moderate risk): 1–2 chronic conditions, care gap closure needs; structured care management touchpoints
  • Tier 4 (Low risk): Preventive care, wellness; automated digital engagement

9.4 Care Management Programs

Disease Management: Structured programs for patients with specific chronic conditions (diabetes, heart failure, COPD, asthma) that provide education, medication adherence support, self-management tools, and regular clinical monitoring. Proven to reduce hospitalisations by 15–30% in well-managed programs.

Case Management: Intensive coordination for patients with complex medical and social needs — typically high-risk tier 1 patients. Case managers work across payer, provider, and community boundaries to coordinate care, remove access barriers, and prevent unnecessary utilisation.

Transitional Care Management: Structured support during the high-risk 30 days following hospital discharge. Includes discharge planning, medication reconciliation, primary care follow-up scheduling, and early warning monitoring. Evidence-based transitional care programs reduce 30-day readmission rates by 20–30%.

Social Determinants of Health (SDoH) Programs: Addressing food insecurity, housing instability, transportation barriers, social isolation, and other non-clinical factors that determine 80% of health outcomes. SDoH programs connect patients to community resources, conduct SDoH screening at clinical touchpoints, and incorporate SDoH data into risk stratification.

9.5 HEDIS Quality Measures and Payer Reporting

HEDIS (Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set) is the most widely used set of performance measures in US healthcare. NCQA (National Committee for Quality Assurance) collects HEDIS data from health plans, and CMS uses HEDIS-derived measures in Medicare Advantage Star Ratings — which directly affect plan payment bonuses and member rebates.

Key HEDIS measures with GCC measurement potential include:

  • Diabetes care: HbA1c control (≤8%), eye exam, kidney health evaluation, blood pressure control
  • Cardiovascular care: Statin therapy for ASCVD, blood pressure control (<140/90), cholesterol management
  • Preventive care: Colorectal cancer screening, breast cancer screening, childhood immunisations, well-child visits
  • Behavioural health: Antidepressant medication management, follow-up after hospitalisation for mental illness

Plans in the top decile of Star performance receive 5% payment bonuses on their Medicare Advantage premium — representing hundreds of millions of dollars for large MA plans. This financial incentive drives significant investment in HEDIS measure improvement programs.

GCC HEDIS operations: Claims-based HEDIS measure calculation, member outreach for care gap closure, provider incentive program analytics, and HEDIS data submission preparation are functions appropriate for GCC delivery.

9.6 Social Determinants of Health — The 80% Factor

Non-clinical social and economic factors — the social determinants of health — account for approximately 80% of health outcomes, yet most clinical systems are designed to address only the clinical 20%. Food insecurity, housing instability, transportation barriers, social isolation, and limited health literacy are the most common SDoH challenges affecting high-risk patient populations.

SDoH screening tools: AHC (Accountable Health Communities) screening tool, PRAPARE (Protocol for Responding to and Assessing Patients' Assets, Risks, and Experiences), and iHELP (Income, Housing, Education, Legal Status, Personal Safety) are commonly used standardised SDoH screening instruments.

SDoH data integration: CMS established Z codes in ICD-10-CM specifically for documenting social determinants (Z55–Z65 code range — problems related to education, employment, housing, family circumstances, etc.). Embedding SDoH screening and Z-code documentation into clinical workflows enables SDoH data to flow into claims for population analytics, payer risk adjustment, and care management targeting.

Community resource integration: Connecting patients with community resources — food banks, housing assistance, transportation programs, legal aid, and mental health services — requires a technology layer that maps patient needs to available community resources. Platforms including findhelp.org (formerly Aunt Bertha), NowPow, and Healthify provide this community resource matching capability.

Chapter 10: Pharmacy Benefit Management & Specialty Pharmacy

Executive Summary

Pharmacy represents the fastest-growing component of healthcare expenditure, driven by specialty drugs — biologics, cell and gene therapies, oncology agents — that can cost $100,000–$4 million per patient course of therapy. PBMs manage pharmacy benefits for over 270 million Americans, controlling formulary design, prior authorisation, rebate negotiation, specialty pharmacy networks, and medication adherence programs. The regulatory environment for PBMs is transforming under the Consolidated Appropriations Act 2026 provisions. This chapter provides the complete PBM and specialty pharmacy operating model.

10.1 PBM Core Functions

Formulary Management: The drug formulary — the list of covered medications organised by tier — is the primary tool for steering prescription behaviour toward clinically appropriate, cost-effective drugs. Formulary committees review clinical evidence, manufacturer rebate offers, and utilisation patterns to design multi-tier formularies that balance access with cost.

Rebate Negotiation: PBMs negotiate rebates from pharmaceutical manufacturers in exchange for formulary placement. These rebates — which can represent 30–50% of a brand drug's list price — are the primary mechanism through which PBMs claim to reduce pharmacy benefit costs. The transparency of rebate pass-through to plan sponsors is a major regulatory and political issue in the CAA 2026 debate.

Prior Authorisation: Specialty drugs and high-cost medications typically require PA to confirm clinical appropriateness before dispensing. PBM PA programs apply clinical criteria (often developed by the PBM's clinical team) to approve or deny pharmacy benefit requests.

Specialty Pharmacy: Specialty drugs require specialised dispensing — temperature-controlled logistics, patient education and support programs, adherence monitoring, and clinical monitoring coordination. PBMs own or contract with specialty pharmacy networks that provide these services.

Medication Adherence Programs: Adherence support — refill reminders, copay assistance coordination, side effect management, and patient education — improves outcomes and reduces downstream medical costs associated with medication non-adherence. Diabetes medication adherence programs, for example, have demonstrated $3,000–$5,000 annual medical cost savings per adherent patient.

10.2 The 340B Program

The 340B Drug Pricing Program requires pharmaceutical manufacturers to provide outpatient drugs to eligible healthcare providers at significantly discounted prices — typically 20–50% below market. 340B-eligible entities include disproportionate share hospitals, FQHCs, Ryan White clinics, and other safety net providers.

340B has grown dramatically — participating hospitals purchase approximately $45 billion in drugs annually under 340B pricing — generating controversy about the use of 340B savings, the definition of eligible patients, and the impact on drug pricing more broadly. The 340B compliance architecture is complex, requiring rigorous tracking of 340B-eligible prescriptions versus non-340B prescriptions to avoid duplicate discounts (which are prohibited).

10.3 Specialty Drug Management

Specialty drugs — biologics, targeted therapies, gene therapies — present the most significant pharmacy cost management challenge. A single CAR-T cell therapy can cost $400,000–$500,000. GLP-1 agonists for obesity and diabetes (semaglutide, tirzepatide) are creating unprecedented pharmacy cost pressure as utilisation has exceeded all projections.

Site-of-care management: Many specialty infusion drugs can be administered in physician offices, ambulatory infusion centres, or at home — at dramatically lower cost than hospital outpatient departments. Site-of-care management programs redirect infusion therapy to lower-cost settings where clinically appropriate.

Biosimilar adoption: As biologic drugs lose patent protection and biosimilars enter the market, PBMs and payers are implementing formulary strategies that incentivise biosimilar adoption. Biosimilars for adalimumab (Humira), etanercept (Enbrel), and bevacizumab (Avastin) offer 30–60% cost savings versus reference products.

Transition management: The transition of a healthcare operations function from a provider or payer's existing team to a GCC is a high-risk event that requires meticulous planning. Best practices include shadow operations (parallel processing during transition), gradual volume ramping (starting at 20% of volume and scaling over 90 days), comprehensive knowledge transfer documentation, and clear escalation paths to client subject matter experts for exception handling.

Chapter 13: Patient Financial Services & Revenue Integrity

Executive Summary

Patient financial responsibility has grown dramatically as high-deductible health plans (HDHPs) have become the dominant insurance design for employer-sponsored coverage. The average individual HDHP deductible reached $1,763 in 2024; family deductibles often exceed $3,500. This shift has made patients a significant payer class in their own right — and has made the patient financial experience a critical competitive and financial variable. This chapter covers patient financial services, price transparency compliance, and revenue integrity.

13.1 Patient Financial Counselling

Financial counselling — the proactive engagement of patients in understanding their financial responsibility before services are rendered — reduces surprise bills, improves point-of-service collections, increases charity care utilisation by eligible patients, and reduces downstream bad debt.

Best-practice financial counselling elements:

  • Insurance verification and benefit explanation in plain language
  • Pre-service cost estimation using the patient's plan and provider contract
  • Financial assistance screening (charity care, Medicaid eligibility, manufacturer assistance programs)
  • Payment plan establishment for patients unable to pay estimated balances
  • Co-pay and deductible collection before service (or prompt scheduling of payment)

Digital financial counselling: AI-powered chatbots and digital pre-registration workflows can perform preliminary financial counselling at scale — presenting patients with cost estimates, financial assistance information, and payment options before they arrive for service. This reduces the burden on financial counsellors and reaches patients who cannot or do not attend pre-service in-person appointments.

13.2 Price Transparency Compliance

The CMS Hospital Price Transparency Rule (effective January 2021, with enforcement escalating since 2022) requires hospitals to publish:

  • A machine-readable file (MRF) of all standard charges — gross charges, payer-specific negotiated rates, and discounted cash prices — for all services
  • A consumer-friendly display of shoppable services with estimated prices

The CMS Transparency in Coverage Rule (effective July 2022) requires health plans to publish MRFs of in-network negotiated rates and out-of-network allowed amounts, and to provide real-time cost estimation tools for members.

Non-compliance with price transparency requirements carries daily civil monetary penalties — up to $2 million annually for large hospitals — and CMS has significantly increased its enforcement activity since 2022.

13.3 Revenue Integrity

Revenue integrity is the discipline of ensuring that all services rendered are accurately charged, correctly coded, appropriately billed, and fully collected — without fraudulent or abusive billing practices. It is the connective tissue between clinical operations and the revenue cycle.

Revenue integrity functions:

  • Charge description master (CDM) maintenance and pricing governance
  • Charge reconciliation (comparing clinical activity documentation to charges captured)
  • Coding quality audits and physician education
  • Charging compliance reviews (OPPS compliance, CPT bundling, CMS quarterly code changes)
  • Revenue opportunity identification (charge capture gaps, undercoded services)
  • Compliance program integration (internal audit, OIG work plan monitoring)

GCC revenue integrity: CDM maintenance, charge audit analytics, and coding quality review are appropriate GCC-deliverable functions. Physician education and compliance remediation require US-based clinical and compliance expertise.

13.4 Self-Pay and Bad Debt Management

Self-pay patients — uninsured patients and insured patients with large deductibles — represent the highest-risk patient financial segment. Best-practice self-pay management includes:

Medicaid presumptive eligibility: Using clinical intake data to presume Medicaid eligibility and begin enrollment before formal application, converting self-pay encounters to Medicaid claims.

Charity care programs: Clearly defined income-based financial assistance policies (required for 501(c)(3) hospitals under IRS and ACA requirements), with proactive screening at registration and at billing.

Payment plans: Flexible, interest-free payment plan options for patients with large balances. Monthly payment plans of $100–$250 are often sustainable for patients who cannot pay large balances upfront.

Early-out collection: Working self-pay balances aggressively in the first 90 days before placing accounts with collection agencies — when collection rates are highest and cost-to-collect is lowest.

Executive Summary

Healthcare analytics has evolved from retrospective financial reporting to real-time operational intelligence and predictive clinical decision support. The healthcare analytics architecture spans five layers, from raw operational data to executive strategic intelligence.

Clinical quality benchmarks: AHRQ patient safety indicators, CMS process and outcome measures, Leapfrog Hospital Safety Grades, and US News & World Report rankings provide clinical quality benchmarking across diagnoses, procedures, and safety domains.

Operational efficiency benchmarks: Productivity standards for coders (charts per hour by encounter type), CDI specialists (charts reviewed per day), denial management specialists (appeals per day), and eligibility verifiers (verifications per hour).

Chapter 15: Compliance, Regulatory Affairs & HIPAA Governance

Executive Summary

Healthcare compliance is not a bureaucratic overlay — it is a material financial and operational risk management function. HIPAA violations, Stark Law and Anti-Kickback Statute exposure, False Claims Act liability, CMS Conditions of Participation deficiencies, and state regulatory actions collectively cost the US healthcare industry billions annually in penalties, settlements, and remediation costs. This chapter provides the compliance architecture for healthcare organisations and GCCs.

15.1 HIPAA Compliance Architecture

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act creates requirements across three rules:

Privacy Rule: Governs the use and disclosure of Protected Health Information (PHI). Patients have rights to access their records, request amendments, and receive an accounting of disclosures. Healthcare organisations must designate a Privacy Officer, develop privacy policies and procedures, train workforce, and establish business associate agreements with vendors who handle PHI.

Security Rule: Requires administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for Electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI). Risk analysis — identifying and assessing risks to ePHI — is the foundational requirement. Technical safeguards include access controls, audit logging, encryption, and transmission security.

Breach Notification Rule: Requires notification to affected individuals, HHS, and (for large breaches) media within 60 days of discovering a breach. Breaches affecting 500+ individuals in a state are publicly posted on HHS's "wall of shame."

For Healthcare GCCs: GCCs processing PHI are Business Associates under HIPAA. BAAs must be executed between the covered entity (US health system or payer) and the GCC. The GCC must implement equivalent HIPAA safeguards — workforce training, access controls, audit logging, incident response — and must report breaches to the covered entity within 60 days.

15.2 Fraud, Waste, and Abuse

False Claims Act: Prohibits submitting false or fraudulent claims to federal healthcare programs (Medicare, Medicaid). Penalties include exclusion from Medicare/Medicaid, treble damages, and per-claim penalties ($13,000–$26,000 per false claim). The FCA whistleblower (qui tam) provision enables employees to file suit on behalf of the government and receive 15–30% of recovered funds.

Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS): Prohibits offering, paying, soliciting, or receiving remuneration in exchange for patient referrals to items or services covered by federal healthcare programs. AKS violations trigger FCA liability and potential exclusion.

Stark Law (Physician Self-Referral Law): Prohibits physician referrals for designated health services to entities with which the physician has a financial relationship, unless a specific exception applies. Unlike AKS, Stark is a strict liability statute — intent is not required.

OIG Compliance Programs: The OIG publishes voluntary compliance program guidance for hospitals, physician practices, and other provider types. OIG compliance programs include written standards and policies, compliance officer designation, effective training, open lines of communication, internal monitoring and auditing, enforcement of standards, and prompt corrective action.

15.3 CMS Conditions of Participation

CMS Conditions of Participation (CoPs) establish the minimum health and safety standards that hospitals, home health agencies, hospices, and other providers must meet to participate in Medicare and Medicaid. CoP surveys are conducted by state survey agencies acting on behalf of CMS and by accreditation organisations (The Joint Commission, DNV).

CoP deficiencies can result in immediate jeopardy findings (requiring immediate corrective action to avoid termination from Medicare), standard-level deficiencies (requiring a Plan of Correction), and civil monetary penalties.

15.4 GCC Compliance Infrastructure

Regulatory monitoring: GCC compliance teams track CMS rule changes (IPPS, OPPS, Physician Fee Schedule), Medicare Advantage regulation updates, state Medicaid waiver changes, and OIG Work Plan priorities — enabling timely updates to operational procedures.

Exclusion screening: OIG and SAM.gov exclusion lists must be checked for all GCC employees and contractors. Monthly automated screening is the standard frequency.

Audit management: GCCs supporting compliance functions conduct pre-payment claim reviews, post-payment coding audits, denial root-cause analysis, and documentation quality reviews — feeding findings into corrective action programs.

Is the GCC's BAA current, and does it accurately reflect the scope of PHI processing activities the GCC performs? Is there an OIG exclusion screening process that covers all GCC employees monthly?

Critical controls: Multi-factor authentication for all remote access, network segmentation between clinical and administrative systems, offline backup systems that cannot be encrypted by ransomware, incident response plans rehearsed annually, and vendor risk management programs.

Chapter 17: Provider Enrollment, Credentialing & Network Management

Executive Summary

Provider enrollment and credentialing are the operational foundation of both billing capability and patient safety assurance. A provider who is not enrolled with a payer cannot bill for services — creating revenue loss during the enrollment gap. A provider who is credentialed without adequate verification creates patient safety and liability risk. This chapter provides the complete operating model for provider enrollment, credentialing, and network management.

17.1 Provider Credentialing: The Safety and Compliance Foundation

Credentialing is the process by which healthcare organisations verify the qualifications, training, licensure, and competency of clinicians before granting them clinical privileges. The legal framework for credentialing includes The Joint Commission accreditation standards, CMS Conditions of Participation, state licensing board requirements, and hospital medical staff bylaws.

Primary Source Verification (PSV): Credentialing standards require verification of qualifications directly from the primary source — the medical school that granted the MD, the state board that issued the license, the NPDB for malpractice history. PSV cannot be delegated to intermediaries or accepted on applicant self-report alone.

CAQH ProView: The Council for Affordable Quality Healthcare (CAQH) ProView database is the industry-standard platform for provider credentialing data maintenance. Providers complete a universal credentialing application in CAQH ProView; participating health plans and hospital systems access the data for credentialing and re-credentialing, reducing duplicative data collection.

Credentialing cycle time: The industry average credentialing cycle time is 90–120 days from application to privileges. Best-practice organisations achieve 60–90 day cycle times through workflow automation, proactive PSV, and electronic committee review. Every day of delay is a day of unbillable provider productivity.

GCC credentialing operations: PSV work — education verification, licensure verification, NPDB queries, malpractice history verification, exclusion screening — is appropriate for GCC delivery. Medical staff committee review and privilege granting are US-based clinical governance functions.

17.2 Provider Enrollment: The Revenue Dependency

Provider enrollment is the process of registering providers with payers to establish billing rights. An enrolled provider can bill the payer for services rendered; an unenrolled provider cannot, regardless of network contract status.

Enrollment complexity: A physician group with 100 providers participating in 50 payer contracts has 5,000 provider-payer enrollment relationships to maintain — each with its own application process, data requirements, processing timeline, and re-enrollment cycle.

Medicare and Medicaid enrollment: CMS enrollment through PECOS (Provider Enrollment, Chain, and Ownership System) is the foundational enrollment for Medicare billing. Medicaid enrollment must be completed separately with each state Medicaid agency for providers billing that state's program.

Revalidation: CMS requires Medicare enrolled providers to revalidate every 3–5 years. Failure to complete revalidation on time results in deactivation of Medicare billing privileges — a revenue risk that requires systematic tracking.

17.3 Provider Network Management

Network adequacy: Payers must maintain provider networks that meet CMS and state-defined adequacy standards — sufficient providers within defined time-and-distance standards for each specialty. Network adequacy failures trigger regulatory action and can disqualify plans from Medicare Advantage and Medicaid managed care participation.

Provider directory accuracy: Payers are required to maintain accurate, current provider directories. Directory errors — incorrect addresses, phone numbers, or network status — create member access problems and regulatory liability. CMS has fined several large health plans for provider directory inaccuracies.

Contracting analytics: Network analytics — identifying geographic coverage gaps, specialty access gaps, and provider performance outliers — inform network development strategy and contract negotiation priorities.

17.4 CAQH ProView and Primary Source Verification

The Council for Affordable Quality Healthcare (CAQH) ProView is the industry standard for provider credentialing data collection and sharing. Over 1.7 million providers maintain CAQH ProView profiles, and over 1,000 health plans and hospitals use CAQH data to streamline credentialing. 17.5 Provider Network Adequacy and Compliance

Network adequacy standards: CMS, state insurance departments, and commercial accreditation bodies (NCQA, URAC) require health plans to maintain provider networks that meet defined access standards — specific time-and-distance standards for primary care and specialists, maximum appointment wait time standards, and minimum provider-to-member ratios. Network adequacy failures result in CMS sanctions, state regulatory actions, and in the Medicare Advantage market, Star rating penalties.

Provider directory accuracy: Provider directories — the lists of in-network providers published for member use — must be accurate and updated within 90 days of provider changes under CMS regulations. Directory inaccuracies generate member complaints, incorrect cost-sharing expectations, and surprise billing disputes. Automated provider data management systems that pull real-time updates from CAQH, state license boards, and NPI registry significantly reduce directory errors.

18.1 Oncology Service Line Operations

Oncology is the most complex and most expensive care domain in US healthcare, with annual cancer care expenditure exceeding $200 billion. The operational complexity of oncology — complex multi-drug regimen management, clinical trial participation, biosimilar formulary navigation, radiation therapy scheduling, and stem cell transplant logistics — requires dedicated administrative infrastructure.

Oncology coding complexity: Oncology coding requires specific expertise — active versus historical cancer status, sequencing of diagnoses for primary versus metastatic disease, chemotherapy administration coding, radiation therapy code families, and surgical oncology procedure coding.

GCC oncology support: Oncology coding, PA management, clinical trial billing compliance, and oncology analytics are appropriate GCC functions with specialised training requirements.

18.2 Behavioural Health Operations

Behavioural health — mental health and substance use disorder services — carries unique billing, coding, and parity compliance requirements. The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) prohibits payers from imposing more restrictive financial requirements or treatment limitations on behavioural health benefits than on medical/surgical benefits. MHPAEA enforcement actions by DOL and state insurance commissioners have increased significantly.

Behavioural health billing complexity: Time-based E/M coding for psychiatric services, psychotherapy coding (add-on codes for combined E/M + psychotherapy), substance use disorder treatment billing (HCPCS H codes for residential and intensive outpatient programs), and MAT (Medication-Assisted Treatment) billing for opioid use disorder.

18.3 Skilled Nursing and Home Health

Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) billing: Medicare Part A pays for SNF stays following a qualifying 3-day inpatient hospitalisation under PDPM (Patient-Driven Payment Model), which classifies patients by clinical characteristics and care needs rather than service volume. SNF billing complexity includes MDS (Minimum Data Set) assessment accuracy, therapy documentation, and the complex rules governing Medicare Part A versus Part B billing transitions.

Home health billing: Home health agencies bill Medicare under the Home Health Resource Group (HHRG) payment system, with payment amounts reflecting patient clinical and functional characteristics captured in the OASIS (Outcome and Assessment Information Set) assessment.

Transitional care management: The 30 days following SNF or home health discharge are the highest-risk period for preventable readmission. Systematic transitional care management — medication reconciliation, primary care follow-up within 7 days, remote monitoring, and care coordinator outreach — reduces readmission rates by 20–30%.

18.5 Home Health and Post-Acute Digital Operations

The post-acute care continuum — from hospital discharge through skilled nursing, home health, outpatient rehabilitation, and long-term care — represents a complex billing environment where GCC operations are increasingly valuable.

PDPM (Patient-Driven Payment Model): SNF billing under PDPM, implemented in 2019, calculates reimbursement based on patient clinical characteristics (diagnoses, functional status, service needs) rather than therapy minutes. PDPM requires accurate assessment coding (MDS coding) and interdisciplinary documentation that supports the clinical characterisation driving payment. MDS coding errors can cost a SNF $50–200 per resident day in lost reimbursement.

Home health OASIS: Home Health billing requires OASIS (Outcome and Assessment Information Set) assessments that determine the episode payment amount under PDGM (Patient-Driven Groupings Model). OASIS accuracy — particularly the clinical assessment items that drive grouping — is a primary driver of home health revenue integrity.

19.2 Clinical Documentation AI

Ambient documentation: AI tools (Nuance DAX Copilot, Abridge, Suki) listen to physician-patient conversations and generate structured clinical notes — eliminating the documentation burden that contributes to physician burnout and drives 2–3 hours of daily after-visit note work.

19.3 Enterprise GenAI Architecture for Healthcare

Data security requirements: All PHI used with LLMs must be processed in HIPAA-compliant environments. Major LLM providers (Microsoft Azure OpenAI Service, Amazon Bedrock, Google Vertex AI) offer HIPAA-eligible deployments with BAAs. Public API access to GPT-4 or Claude is not HIPAA-compliant for PHI processing.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): Healthcare LLM applications typically use RAG architecture — retrieving relevant clinical policies, payer rules, or medical evidence before generating responses. RAG grounds LLM outputs in the organisation's specific policies and current clinical evidence, reducing hallucination risk.

Human-in-the-loop requirements: All GenAI outputs in clinical and billing contexts must be reviewed by qualified professionals before use. PA letters must be reviewed by clinical staff. Denial appeals must be reviewed by coding or clinical professionals. AI-generated clinical notes must be reviewed and authenticated by the responsible physician.

Hallucination management: LLMs can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect clinical or billing information. For healthcare applications, output validation — checking generated content against source documents, policy databases, and structured clinical data — is essential before human review.

19.4 GCC GenAI Delivery Model

Healthcare GCCs are the natural implementation and operations centres for enterprise GenAI programs:

AI operations: GCC teams manage the human-in-the-loop workflows — reviewing AI-generated PA letters, appeal letters, and documentation summaries before they are released to production use.

Quality monitoring: Ongoing monitoring of AI output quality — tracking accuracy, hallucination rates, and clinical appropriateness — against defined performance standards.

Chapter 21: Payment Integrity, FWA & Audit Analytics

Executive Summary

Payment integrity is the discipline of ensuring healthcare claims are paid correctly — not overpaid, not underpaid, and not fraudulently obtained. The scale of the problem is staggering: the federal government estimates that healthcare fraud, waste, and abuse costs $60–90 billion annually across Medicare and Medicaid alone. Improper payments in Medicare FFS totalled $31.4 billion in FY2023 per CMS reporting. Commercial payers face equivalent proportional losses. Payment integrity programs — combining pre-payment editing, post-payment audit, predictive analytics, and investigation — are among the highest-ROI investments in healthcare operations, returning $5–12 for every dollar invested.

21.1 The Payment Integrity Ecosystem

Payment integrity operates across three time horizons:

Pre-payment controls catch errors and anomalies before money leaves the payer. This includes claim editing (checking for coding validity, medical necessity, duplicate submission, coverage eligibility), clinical review (authorisation confirmation, medical necessity evaluation), and AI-based anomaly detection that flags statistically unusual billing patterns. Pre-payment controls are the highest-value intervention — stopping a fraudulent payment costs a fraction of recovering it.

Real-time payment controls apply at the moment of adjudication — automated flags that pause specific claims for review based on real-time risk scoring, provider behaviour patterns, or diagnosis-procedure combination anomalies.

Post-payment controls recover funds after disbursement through audit programs, retrospective review, data mining, coordination of benefits recovery, and subrogation. While harder than pre-payment prevention, post-payment recovery remains valuable — the average post-payment audit program identifies 1–3% of total paid claims as overpayments.

21.2 Fraud, Waste & Abuse Taxonomy

Fraud involves intentional deception to obtain unauthorised payment: billing for services not rendered, upcoding, unbundling, identity theft, phantom providers, and kickback arrangements. Fraud is a federal crime under the False Claims Act, Anti-Kickback Statute, and healthcare fraud statutes.

Waste involves unnecessary resource consumption without intentional deception: overutilisation, inefficient care delivery, administrative inefficiency, and excessive resource consumption due to poorly designed care protocols.

Abuse falls between fraud and waste: billing practices that are inconsistent with professional standards or payer policy but not necessarily intentional fraud. Examples include inappropriate modifier use, systematic overbilling of E&M levels, and routine waiver of patient cost share.

21.3 High-Risk Fraud Schemes

Phantom billing: Claims for services never provided. Highest prevalence in home health, DME, and mental health services.

Upcoding and DRG gaming: Systematic billing at higher payment levels than clinical documentation supports. In DRG-based inpatient billing, every MS-DRG level represents a specific clinical and financial threshold — upcoding adds complications or comorbidities that were not clinically present.

Unbundling: Billing component codes separately when a comprehensive code should apply. CPT's National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) edits address this systematically, but unapproved modifier use can bypass them.

Kickback arrangements: Financial inducements for referrals — the most common federal prosecution basis in healthcare, affecting both the paying and receiving parties.

Identity theft and credential fraud: Billing under stolen provider or member identities. Account takeover in the healthcare context can generate millions in fraudulent claims before detection.

21.4 Analytics Architecture for Payment Integrity

Rule-based editing applies deterministic checks — NCCI edits, LCD/NCD medical necessity rules, age-sex validity, anatomical consistency. High throughput, low cost, well-understood. But rules can only catch what they were designed to catch — sophisticated schemes route around known edits.

Statistical anomaly detection compares provider billing patterns to peer benchmarks. A provider billing E&M level 5 (the highest complexity visit code) for 95% of encounters when the peer average is 25% is a statistical outlier that warrants investigation. Provider profiling, peer benchmarking, and Z-score analysis are standard tools.

Machine learning models learn complex non-linear relationships between billing patterns, clinical context, provider characteristics, and fraud probability. Gradient boosted models trained on confirmed fraud cases outperform rule-based systems for catching novel schemes. Models must be continuously retrained as fraud patterns evolve.

Network analysis maps relationships between providers, patients, and billing entities to identify coordinated fraud rings — groups of providers who systematically refer to each other, share patients, or use the same billing entity for implausible service combinations.

NLP and document analysis extracts clinical content from medical records to detect documentation that does not support the billed services — detecting copy-paste, template abuse, and fabricated clinical notes.

21.5 GCC Opportunity in Payment Integrity

Payment integrity analytics and audit operations are among the highest-value GCC functions in healthcare. Data analysis, coding review, audit letter generation, provider education, and coordination of benefits recovery are all structured, high-volume activities well-suited to GCC delivery.

Leading healthcare GCCs in India run dedicated payment integrity operations that include: claims audit and review teams processing 2,000–5,000 claims per FTE per month; coding review specialists validating inpatient DRG assignments and outpatient APC groupings; provider profiling analysts generating peer benchmark reports; and coordination of benefits recovery teams working commercial-commercial, Medicare-secondary, and subrogation claims.

ROI benchmark: Well-designed payment integrity GCC operations return $8–15 per dollar of GCC operating cost, making them among the highest-ROI GCC investments in healthcare.

Chapter 22: Contact Centers — Member & Patient Services Operations

Executive Summary

Healthcare contact centres are the primary human interface between patients, members, and their healthcare system. They handle scheduling, eligibility inquiries, prior authorisation status, billing questions, appeals, care navigation, medication refills, and crisis support — across multiple channels (phone, chat, portal, SMS) and multiple audiences (patients, members, providers, caregivers). Contact centre performance directly drives CAHPS scores, member satisfaction, and Star ratings — making it simultaneously an operational function and a strategic asset. AI and digital transformation are reshaping what healthcare contact centres can do, at what cost, and at what quality level.

22.1 Contact Centre Scope in Healthcare

Healthcare contact centres serve two distinct customer groups with overlapping but distinct needs:

Patient-facing contact centres at provider organisations handle appointment scheduling, pre-registration completion, billing inquiries, financial counselling referrals, medical records requests, prescription refill routing, clinical question triage to clinical staff, and post-discharge follow-up. Patient experience in these interactions drives satisfaction scores that influence referral decisions and, in value-based care models, quality bonus payments.

Member-facing contact centres at payer organisations handle eligibility verification, benefits explanation, prior authorisation status inquiry, claims status and dispute, provider directory assistance, referral management, care management programme enrolment, grievance and appeals intake, and wellness programme support. Medicare Advantage Star ratings include CAHPS-based member experience measures that directly affect plan revenue — a one-star improvement in a plan serving 100,000 members can generate $30–50 million in additional CMS payments.

Provider-facing contact centres at payers handle provider portal support, credentialing status, claims and payment inquiries, prior authorisation intake, and network participation questions. Provider satisfaction with payer administrative processes affects network participation decisions — providers who experience high administrative burden are more likely to leave networks.

22.2 The AI-Transformed Contact Centre

The healthcare contact centre of 2025 looks fundamentally different from the contact centre of 2019. AI has transformed every layer of the contact centre operating model:

Conversational AI and IVR: Natural language IVR systems (replacing touch-tone DTMF menus) allow callers to state their need in natural speech — "I need to know if my procedure is covered" or "What's the status of my claim" — and route intelligently to the appropriate agent or self-service flow. Azure Bot Service, Google CCAI, and Nuance Conversational AI are leading platforms.

Real-time agent assist: AI tools that listen to calls in real time and surface relevant information for agents — the patient's account, benefit details, prior authorisation history, relevant policy language — without agents having to navigate multiple systems during the call. Demonstrated to reduce average handle time by 20–35% and improve first-call resolution by 15–25%.

Post-call summarisation: Generative AI tools that produce accurate call summaries immediately after call completion, eliminating 2–5 minutes of after-call work per interaction and improving documentation consistency.

Sentiment analysis and QA: AI-powered quality assurance that evaluates 100% of calls (versus the traditional 2–5% manual sampling) for adherence to scripts, regulatory requirements, and service standards. Real-time sentiment analysis flags distressed callers for supervisor intervention.

Predictive routing: ML models that predict which agent is most likely to successfully resolve a specific caller's issue based on the caller's history, query type, language preference, and agent specialisation — routing for first-call resolution rather than just for availability.

22.3 GCC Model for Contact Centre Operations

Healthcare contact centres were among the first healthcare functions to move to GCC/offshore models, and they remain the highest-volume GCC function by headcount. India, the Philippines, and increasingly Eastern Europe host large healthcare contact centre operations serving US payer and provider clients.

Why GCCs work for contact centres:

  • High volume, structured workflows with clear decision trees
  • Trainable to healthcare-specific requirements (HIPAA, payer-specific rules, clinical triage protocols)
  • Significant labour cost arbitrage (40–55% cost reduction versus US equivalents)
  • Scalability — GCCs can ramp headcount for open enrolment seasons, product launches, and demand surges

GCC maturity levels for contact centres:

  • Entry: Overflow handling, after-hours scheduling, basic eligibility inquiry
  • Intermediate: Claims status, prior auth status, billing inquiries, appointment management
  • Advanced: Complex benefits counselling, care navigation, financial counselling, grievance intake, multi-lingual support

Governance requirements: HIPAA BAA, PHI access controls, call recording and storage compliance, real-time monitoring, QA sampling, and escalation protocols to onshore clinical staff for clinical questions.

23.6 Dashboard Design Principles

Single source of truth: Every metric must have a defined calculation methodology, a defined data source, and a defined owner. Ambiguous metrics are gamed or ignored.

Daily operational metrics, weekly management metrics, monthly board metrics: Frequency must match the decision cycle of the audience.

Leading plus lagging: For every lagging indicator (denial rate last month), include a leading indicator that predicts its future trajectory (eligibility verification rate this week).

Cause and effect visualisation: The most effective healthcare dashboards connect root cause metrics to outcome metrics visually — showing that a drop in eligibility verification rate leads, with a 30-day lag, to an increase in eligibility-related denial rate.

Benchmark context: Every metric is most meaningful relative to a benchmark — internal historical trend, peer group average, or best-in-class target. A 10% denial rate means nothing without knowing whether the benchmark is 8% or 15%.
